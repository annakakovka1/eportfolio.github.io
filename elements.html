Machine Learning

This postgraduate module introduces machine learning through a practical, real-world data analytics focus. It develops core theoretical understanding alongside hands-on experience with modern tools such as Python or R. The module covers the design and evaluation of machine learning models, current methods and deployment practices, and the challenges of applying machine learning in professional contexts.

Learning Outcomes

I will learn:

  • Critical key machine learning paradigms, algorithms, and their theoretical foundations.
  • Analyse and interpret complex datasets, selecting appropriate machine learning techniques for different problem contexts.
  • Design, implement, and evaluate machine learning models using modern programming environments such as Python or R.
  • Critically appraise the performance, limitations, and risks of machine learning solutions in real-world scenarios.

Discussion: Initial Post: Rethinking Digital Resilience in Healthcare—Lessons from the HSE Cyberattack

Industry 4.0 has accelerated the digital transformation of sectors such as healthcare through interconnected systems, automation, and data-driven decision-making. However, as Metcalf (2024) suggests, the transition toward Industry 5.0 introduces a critical shift prioritising resilience, human oversight, and ethical responsibility alongside technological advancement. This distinction is particularly important when evaluating real-world system failures, where the limits of purely technology-driven models become evident.

A compelling example is the 2021 ransomware attack on the Irish Health Service Executive (HSE). While digitalisation had improved efficiency through centralised electronic health records and networked hospital systems, the attack exposed a fundamental weakness: an over-reliance on highly integrated IT infrastructures that lacked sufficient resilience. The immediate shutdown of systems led to cancelled appointments, delayed cancer treatments, and a reversion to paper-based processes (HSE, 2021). This highlights a critical contradiction within Industry 4.0: while integration enhances performance, it also creates single points of failure.

Critically, the incident demonstrates that technological sophistication does not necessarily equate to system robustness. The economic cost, estimated at over €100 million (Department of Health Ireland, 2022), is significant, but arguably the more profound impacts lie in patient safety risks and the erosion of public trust. From a data perspective, the attack also raises questions about dataset governance in machine learning-enabled healthcare systems; compromised or inaccessible data undermines the reliability of algorithmic decision-making, limiting the effectiveness of AI tools.

From an Industry 5.0 standpoint, this failure suggests that organisations have not yet fully embraced resilience-oriented design. A more human-centric approach would prioritise fail-safes, decentralisation, and staff preparedness, rather than assuming uninterrupted system availability. In hindsight, the HSE’s reliance on legacy systems and insufficient cybersecurity investment reflects organisational and managerial shortcomings, not just technical ones.

In conclusion, the HSE cyberattack illustrates that the benefits of Industry 4.0 technologies are contingent on robust governance, security, and human-centred design. Industry 5.0 offers a necessary corrective lens, emphasising that sustainable innovation requires balancing efficiency with resilience and trust.

Jaccard Coefficient Analysis and Critical Reflection in Machine Learning

This activity applied the Jaccard coefficient to compare categorical pathological test results for three individuals: Jack, Mary and Jim. In machine learning, similarity measures are valuable in tasks such as clustering, classification and pattern recognition, particularly when structured categorical variables must be compared systematically.

Purpose of the Analysis

The aim of this exercise was to calculate similarity across pathological test results and then critically reflect on the wider implications of data quality, ethics, legal responsibility and professional judgement in machine learning practice.

Dataset

Name Gender Fever Cough Test-1 Test-2 Test-3 Test-4
Jack M Y N P N N A
Mary F Y N P A P N
Jim M Y P N N N A

The similarity calculation was based on six variables: Fever, Cough, Test-1, Test-2, Test-3 and Test-4. Name and gender were excluded because they do not directly contribute to the pathological comparison.

Key Finding

The analysis shows that Jack and Jim are the most similar pair with a Jaccard coefficient of 0.67, while Jim and Mary are the least similar with a coefficient of 0.17. This provides a useful introductory example of how categorical similarity can be measured, but it also illustrates how easily complex data can be reduced to simplified numerical outputs.

Critical Reflection

Dataset Challenges

This dataset is extremely small, containing only three individuals. In machine learning, such limited data is not representative and cannot support robust generalisation. Small datasets increase the risk of unreliable findings, poor model behaviour and biased conclusions.

Methodological Limitations

The Jaccard coefficient treats all variables as equally important and all mismatches as equivalent. In a medical context, however, some pathological tests may be more clinically significant than others. This limits the interpretive depth of the method.

Ethical Considerations

Healthcare data is highly sensitive. Even a simple similarity exercise raises ethical questions around privacy, fairness, transparency and the risk of misinterpreting algorithmic outputs without appropriate human oversight.

Legal Responsibility

In real healthcare applications, medical data would fall under data protection regulation and require lawful collection, secure storage and responsible processing. Mishandling such data could lead to legal consequences and loss of trust.

Professional Practice

Machine learning professionals must justify why a method is appropriate, explain its limitations clearly and avoid presenting similarity scores as definitive truth. Technical outputs must always be supported by critical judgement and domain awareness.

Overall Evaluation

This exercise demonstrates that machine learning is not only about computation. It also requires reflection on data quality, human impact, governance, accountability and the wider implications of automated or semi-automated decision support.

Overall, the Jaccard coefficient is a useful introductory measure for categorical similarity, but its application in sensitive domains such as healthcare must be approached critically. This task reinforced that responsible machine learning practice depends not only on mathematical accuracy, but also on ethical awareness, legal compliance and professional responsibility.

Perceptron Activities (Artificial Neural Networks): Dataset Applicability and Critical Analysis

This portfolio activity explored three introductory artificial neural network tasks: a simple perceptron, a perceptron trained on the AND logic gate, and a multi-layer perceptron (MLP). The purpose was to understand how dataset characteristics influence algorithm selection, model behaviour and the quality of predictive outcomes. Across the exercises, the key learning outcome was that machine learning performance depends not only on the algorithm itself, but also on whether the dataset is linearly separable, noisy, small, balanced and suitable for the model being used.

Learning Outcome Addressed

This activity demonstrates understanding of the applicability and challenges associated with different datasets for machine learning algorithms by running perceptron-based models and critically analysing their performance, limitations and suitability.

Code Explanation and Model Logic

Simple Perceptron Implementation

The simple perceptron notebook focused on the standard perceptron learning process. The model begins by initialising weights and a bias term, then repeatedly updates them based on prediction errors across the training data.

weights = np.zeros(n_features)
bias = 0
learning_rate = 0.1

for epoch in range(epochs):
    for x_i, y_i in zip(X, y):
        linear_output = np.dot(x_i, weights) + bias
        y_pred = 1 if linear_output >= 0 else 0
        update = learning_rate * (y_i - y_pred)
        weights += update * x_i
        bias += update

In practical terms, np.dot(x_i, weights) + bias computes the weighted sum of the inputs, the threshold converts that sum into a binary prediction, and the update rule adjusts the parameters whenever the model classifies an example incorrectly. This process continues until the model converges or reaches the maximum number of iterations.

AND Gate Perceptron

The AND gate notebook used the perceptron on a truth table with four observations. This is one of the clearest examples of linearly separable data because only the input pair (1,1) belongs to the positive class.

X = np.array([[0,0], [0,1], [1,0], [1,1]])
y = np.array([0, 0, 0, 1])

perceptron.fit(X, y)
predictions = perceptron.predict(X)

This code defines the dataset explicitly, trains the model and then tests whether the learned weights correctly classify each input. Because the data is linearly separable, the perceptron is able to converge and produce the correct output for all four combinations.

Multi-Layer Perceptron

The multi-layer perceptron notebook moved beyond the limitations of a single-layer model by introducing hidden neurons and non-linear activation functions.

from sklearn.neural_network import MLPClassifier

model = MLPClassifier(
    hidden_layer_sizes=(5,),
    activation='relu',
    max_iter=1000
)

model.fit(X_train, y_train)
predictions = model.predict(X_test)

Here, the hidden layer enables the model to capture more complex patterns than a simple perceptron can learn. The fitting stage uses backpropagation to reduce prediction error iteratively, and the final predictions are tested on unseen data to assess generalisation.

Visual Interpretation of Results

Decision Boundary: Simple Perceptron

Suggested figure: Scatter plot of the two classes with a straight-line decision boundary.

Interpretation: A successful perceptron creates a clear straight line separating the classes. If the dataset is truly linearly separable, most or all points will appear on the correct side of the line. If points overlap or require a curved boundary, the simple perceptron will misclassify some observations.

AND Gate Classification Plot

Suggested figure: A 2D plot of the four input combinations, highlighting that only (1,1) is classified as 1.

Interpretation: This visualisation shows why the AND dataset is suitable for a perceptron. A single straight-line boundary can isolate the positive case from the negative cases. This confirms the theory that perceptrons are effective on linearly separable problems.

MLP Loss Curve and Accuracy

Suggested figure: Loss curve over training epochs, and a comparison of training and testing accuracy.

Interpretation: A steadily decreasing loss curve indicates that the model is learning effectively. If training accuracy is high but testing accuracy is much lower, this may suggest overfitting. When both remain reasonably strong, the model is more likely to generalise well to new data.

Critical Result Summary

The simple perceptron and AND gate activities demonstrate that single-layer networks perform well when datasets are linearly separable and low in complexity. The MLP activity shows that more advanced architectures are necessary for handling non-linear datasets, but this added capability also introduces greater computational cost, more hyperparameter choices and an increased risk of overfitting.

Critical Analysis of Dataset Applicability

Linearly Separable Data

Simple perceptrons are highly suitable for linearly separable datasets because they learn a single linear decision boundary efficiently. The AND gate is a strong example of this and illustrates fast convergence with minimal computational cost.

Non-Linear Data

When the dataset is not linearly separable, a single-layer perceptron becomes inadequate. In such cases, an MLP is more appropriate because hidden layers enable the model to represent more complex and non-linear patterns.

Dataset Size and Simplicity

The small synthetic datasets used in these activities are excellent for learning foundational concepts, but they do not reflect the messiness of real-world data. As a result, their value is educational rather than predictive or broadly generalisable.

Noise and Data Quality

In practice, real datasets often include noise, missing values and imbalance. These issues can reduce performance, distort the decision boundary and make evaluation more difficult. This means preprocessing and careful validation are essential.

Model Complexity and Risk

Although MLPs are more flexible, they require greater tuning, more training time and closer evaluation. Without appropriate parameter selection, a model may overfit training data and perform poorly on unseen examples.

Professional Judgement

These exercises reinforced that choosing a model is not just a technical task. It requires critical judgement about the nature of the dataset, the complexity of the problem and the trade-off between interpretability, accuracy and computational efficiency.

Reflection

This activity provided practical insight into how machine learning models behave when exposed to different types of datasets. By implementing and analysing the simple perceptron, the AND gate perceptron and the multi-layer perceptron, I developed a clearer understanding of both the strengths and limitations of neural network models.

One of the most important lessons learned was the significance of dataset structure. Initially, I assumed that a single model could be applied universally; however, through experimentation, it became clear that the simple perceptron only performs well on linearly separable data. For example, the AND gate dataset produced perfect results because it can be separated by a straight line. This reinforced the theoretical concept that perceptrons rely on linear decision boundaries.

A key challenge I encountered was understanding why the perceptron fails on more complex datasets. This led me to explore the limitations of linear models and appreciate the need for hidden layers and non-linear activation functions in multi-layer perceptrons. Implementing the MLP helped me see how additional layers improve model flexibility, although it also introduced new difficulties such as longer training times and the need for parameter tuning.

Another important reflection is related to model evaluation. Running the code alone was not sufficient; interpreting outputs such as accuracy and loss curves required critical thinking. I learned that high accuracy does not always indicate a good model, especially if overfitting occurs. This emphasised the importance of comparing training and testing performance rather than relying on a single metric.

In terms of skills development, this activity improved my ability to read and understand machine learning code, interpret model outputs and visualisations, and critically evaluate the suitability of algorithms for different datasets. If I were to extend this work, I would apply the models to more realistic datasets, experiment with different hyperparameters and compare perceptrons with other machine learning methods such as decision trees or support vector machines.

Overall, this activity strengthened my understanding of how machine learning models interact with data and highlighted the importance of critical analysis rather than simply running algorithms. It showed that successful machine learning depends not only on implementation, but on informed judgement about the dataset, the model and the meaning of the results.

Initial Post-Robo-Writers and Epistemic Risk: A Critical Evaluation of AI-Generated Text in Socio-Technical Contexts

The article by Matthew Hutson (2021) in Nature highlights the rapid advancement of AI-generated text systems (“robo-writers”) and their growing application across domains. These systems, often powered by large language models, present both substantial benefits and notable risks depending on their level of use.

At the administrative level, AI writers offer clear efficiency gains. Tasks such as report drafting, email composition, and summarisation can be automated, reducing cognitive load and operational costs (Davenport & Ronanki, 2018). This aligns with broader machine learning applications aimed at augmenting human productivity. However, risks arise from over-reliance, particularly where outputs are accepted uncritically. Errors, hallucinations, or biases embedded in training data can propagate into decision-making processes (Bender et al., 2021). In high-stakes environments, this introduces technical uncertainty and potential accountability gaps.

At intermediate levels, such as journalism or academic writing support, AI systems can enhance creativity and speed but may blur authorship boundaries. Hutson (2021) notes concern about misinformation and the erosion of trust in written content. This reflects wider debates about epistemic reliability in AI-generated knowledge (Floridi & Chiriatti, 2020).

In creative writing, AI offers novel collaborative possibilities, enabling co-creation and experimentation beyond human limitations. Yet, it raises ethical concerns regarding originality, intellectual property, and cultural homogenisation (Elgammal et al., 2017). The risk is not only technical but also socio-cultural, as machine-generated narratives may replicate dominant patterns in training data.

Critically, the application of AI writers must include risk mitigation strategies, such as transparency mechanisms, human oversight, and dataset auditing. While AI can augment human capabilities, its deployment without robust governance frameworks may undermine trust, creativity, and accountability. Thus, balancing innovation with ethical safeguards is essential in real-world machine learning applications.
