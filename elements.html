Discussion: Initial Post: Rethinking Digital Resilience in Healthcare—Lessons from the HSE Cyberattack

Industry 4.0 has accelerated the digital transformation of sectors such as healthcare through interconnected systems, automation, and data-driven decision-making. However, as Metcalf (2024) suggests, the transition toward Industry 5.0 introduces a critical shift prioritising resilience, human oversight, and ethical responsibility alongside technological advancement.

A compelling example is the 2021 ransomware attack on the Irish Health Service Executive (HSE). While digitalisation had improved efficiency through centralised electronic health records and networked hospital systems, the attack exposed a fundamental weakness: an over-reliance on highly integrated IT infrastructures that lacked sufficient resilience. The immediate shutdown of systems led to cancelled appointments, delayed cancer treatments, and a reversion to paper-based processes (Health Service Executive, 2021).

Critically, the incident demonstrates that technological sophistication does not necessarily equate to system robustness. The financial impact was subsequently reported by the Comptroller and Auditor General as approaching €100 million in direct and additional ICT-related expenditure, underlining the scale of organisational disruption (Comptroller and Auditor General, 2022). From a data perspective, the attack also raises questions about dataset governance in machine learning-enabled healthcare systems; compromised or inaccessible data undermines the reliability of algorithmic decision-making, limiting the effectiveness of AI tools.

From an Industry 5.0 standpoint, this failure suggests that organisations have not yet fully embraced resilience-oriented design. A more human-centric approach would prioritise fail-safes, decentralisation, and staff preparedness, rather than assuming uninterrupted system availability. In hindsight, the HSE’s reliance on legacy systems and insufficient cybersecurity investment reflects organisational and managerial shortcomings, not just technical ones (Metcalf, 2024).

In conclusion, the HSE cyberattack illustrates that the benefits of Industry 4.0 technologies are contingent on robust governance, security, and human-centred design. Industry 5.0 offers a necessary corrective lens, emphasising that sustainable innovation requires balancing efficiency with resilience and trust.

Jaccard Coefficient Analysis and Critical Reflection in Machine Learning

This activity applied the Jaccard coefficient to compare categorical pathological test results for three individuals: Jack, Mary and Jim. In machine learning, similarity measures are valuable in tasks such as clustering, classification and pattern recognition, particularly when structured categorical variables must be compared systematically (Jaccard, 1901; Han, Kamber and Pei, 2012; Tan, Steinbach and Kumar, 2019).

Purpose of the Analysis

The aim of this exercise was to calculate similarity across pathological test results and then critically reflect on the wider implications of data quality, ethics, legal responsibility and professional judgement in machine learning practice.

Key Finding

The analysis shows that Jack and Jim are the most similar pair with a Jaccard coefficient of 0.67, while Jim and Mary are the least similar with a coefficient of 0.17. This provides a useful introductory example of how categorical similarity can be measured, but it also illustrates how easily complex data can be reduced to simplified numerical outputs.

Critical Reflection

Dataset Challenges

This dataset is extremely small, containing only three individuals. In machine learning, such limited data is not representative and cannot support robust generalisation. Small datasets increase the risk of unreliable findings, poor model behaviour and biased conclusions (Bishop, 2006; Kelleher, Mac Namee and D’Arcy, 2020).

Methodological Limitations

The Jaccard coefficient treats all variables as equally important and all mismatches as equivalent. In a medical context, however, some pathological tests may be more clinically significant than others. This limits the interpretive depth of the method (Han, Kamber and Pei, 2012).

Ethical Considerations

Healthcare data is highly sensitive. Even a simple similarity exercise raises ethical questions around privacy, fairness, transparency and the risk of misinterpreting algorithmic outputs without appropriate human oversight (Floridi et al., 2018).

Legal Responsibility

In real healthcare applications, medical data would fall under data protection regulation and require lawful collection, secure storage and responsible processing. Mishandling such data could lead to legal consequences and loss of trust (European Parliament and Council of the European Union, 2016).

Professional Practice

Machine learning professionals must justify why a method is appropriate, explain its limitations clearly and avoid presenting similarity scores as definitive truth. Technical outputs must always be supported by critical judgement and domain awareness (Kelleher, Mac Namee and D’Arcy, 2020).

Overall Evaluation

This exercise demonstrates that machine learning is not only about computation. It also requires reflection on data quality, human impact, governance, accountability and the wider implications of automated or semi-automated decision support.

Overall, the Jaccard coefficient is a useful introductory measure for categorical similarity, but its application in sensitive domains such as healthcare must be approached critically. This task reinforced that responsible machine learning practice depends not only on mathematical accuracy, but also on ethical awareness, legal compliance and professional responsibility.

Perceptron Activities (Artificial Neural Networks): Dataset Applicability and Critical Analysis

This portfolio activity explored three introductory artificial neural network tasks: a simple perceptron, a perceptron trained on the AND logic gate, and a multi-layer perceptron (MLP). The purpose was to understand how dataset characteristics influence algorithm selection, model behaviour and the quality of predictive outcomes. Across the exercises, the key learning outcome was that machine learning performance depends not only on the algorithm itself, but also on whether the dataset is linearly separable, noisy, small, balanced and suitable for the model being used (Rosenblatt, 1958; Bishop, 2006; Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville, 2016).

Learning Outcome Addressed

This activity demonstrates understanding of the applicability and challenges associated with different datasets for machine learning algorithms by running perceptron-based models and critically analysing their performance, limitations and suitability.

Code Explanation and Model Logic

Simple Perceptron Implementation

The simple perceptron notebook focused on the standard perceptron learning process originally proposed by Rosenblatt (1958). The model begins by initialising weights and a bias term, then repeatedly updates them based on prediction errors across the training data.

weights = np.zeros(n_features)
bias = 0
learning_rate = 0.1

for epoch in range(epochs):
    for x_i, y_i in zip(X, y):
        linear_output = np.dot(x_i, weights) + bias
        y_pred = 1 if linear_output >= 0 else 0
        update = learning_rate * (y_i - y_pred)
        weights += update * x_i
        bias += update

In practical terms, np.dot(x_i, weights) + bias computes the weighted sum of the inputs, the threshold converts that sum into a binary prediction, and the update rule adjusts the parameters whenever the model classifies an example incorrectly. This process continues until the model converges or reaches the maximum number of iterations (Bishop, 2006).

AND Gate Perceptron

The AND gate notebook used the perceptron on a truth table with four observations. This is one of the clearest examples of linearly separable data because only the input pair (1,1) belongs to the positive class (Rosenblatt, 1958).

X = np.array([[0,0], [0,1], [1,0], [1,1]])
y = np.array([0, 0, 0, 1])

perceptron.fit(X, y)
predictions = perceptron.predict(X)

This code defines the dataset explicitly, trains the model and then tests whether the learned weights correctly classify each input. Because the data is linearly separable, the perceptron is able to converge and produce the correct output for all four combinations.

Multi-Layer Perceptron

The multi-layer perceptron notebook moved beyond the limitations of a single-layer model by introducing hidden neurons and non-linear activation functions (Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville, 2016).

from sklearn.neural_network import MLPClassifier

model = MLPClassifier(
    hidden_layer_sizes=(5,),
    activation='relu',
    max_iter=1000
)

model.fit(X_train, y_train)
predictions = model.predict(X_test)

Here, the hidden layer enables the model to capture more complex patterns than a simple perceptron can learn. The fitting stage uses backpropagation to reduce prediction error iteratively, and the final predictions are tested on unseen data to assess generalisation (Géron, 2019).

Critical Result Summary

The simple perceptron and AND gate activities demonstrate that single-layer networks perform well when datasets are linearly separable and low in complexity. The MLP activity shows that more advanced architectures are necessary for handling non-linear datasets, but this added capability also introduces greater computational cost, more hyperparameter choices and an increased risk of overfitting (Bishop, 2006; Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville, 2016).

Critical Analysis of Dataset Applicability

Linearly Separable Data

Simple perceptrons are highly suitable for linearly separable datasets because they learn a single linear decision boundary efficiently. The AND gate is a strong example of this and illustrates fast convergence with minimal computational cost (Rosenblatt, 1958).

Non-Linear Data

When the dataset is not linearly separable, a single-layer perceptron becomes inadequate. In such cases, an MLP is more appropriate because hidden layers enable the model to represent more complex and non-linear patterns (Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville, 2016).

Dataset Size and Simplicity

The small synthetic datasets used in these activities are excellent for learning foundational concepts, but they do not reflect the messiness of real-world data. As a result, their value is educational rather than predictive or broadly generalisable (Bishop, 2006).

Noise and Data Quality

In practice, real datasets often include noise, missing values and imbalance. These issues can reduce performance, distort the decision boundary and make evaluation more difficult. This means preprocessing and careful validation are essential (Géron, 2019).

Model Complexity and Risk

Although MLPs are more flexible, they require greater tuning, more training time and closer evaluation. Without appropriate parameter selection, a model may overfit training data and perform poorly on unseen examples (Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville, 2016).

Professional Judgement

These exercises reinforced that choosing a model is not just a technical task. It requires critical judgement about the nature of the dataset, the complexity of the problem and the trade-off between interpretability, accuracy and computational efficiency.

AUC Analysis: Logistic Regression

For the classification experiment, logistic regression was applied to a standard binary classification dataset. The main parameter varied was C, which controls the strength of regularisation. Smaller values impose stronger regularisation, while larger values allow the model to fit the data more freely (James et al., 2021).

from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.metrics import roc_auc_score

for c in [0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 100]:
    model = LogisticRegression(C=c, max_iter=5000)
    model.fit(X_train, y_train)
    y_prob = model.predict_proba(X_test)[:, 1]
    auc = roc_auc_score(y_test, y_prob)
    print(c, auc)

Key AUC Finding

The best AUC was achieved at C = 1. Increasing model flexibility improved AUC up to a point, but very large values did not produce better classification and led to a slight decline. This shows that stronger complexity does not always lead to stronger generalisation (Provost and Fawcett, 2013).

R² Analysis: Ridge Regression

For the regression experiment, Ridge regression was used to evaluate the impact of the alpha parameter on model fit. Here, alpha controls the strength of L2 regularisation. Small alpha values apply light regularisation, while large alpha values strongly constrain the model and can lead to underfitting (James et al., 2021; Kuhn and Johnson, 2019).

from sklearn.linear_model import Ridge
from sklearn.metrics import r2_score, mean_squared_error
import numpy as np

for alpha in [0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 100]:
    model = Ridge(alpha=alpha)
    model.fit(X_train, y_train)
    pred = model.predict(X_test)
    r2 = r2_score(y_test, pred)
    rmse = np.sqrt(mean_squared_error(y_test, pred))
    print(alpha, r2, rmse)

Key R² Finding

The best R² result was observed at alpha = 0.1. As alpha increased further, R² decreased sharply and RMSE worsened, showing a clear underfitting pattern. This demonstrates that regularisation must be tuned carefully because too much constraint can remove useful predictive structure from the model.

Initial Post-Robo-Writers and Epistemic Risk: A Critical Evaluation of AI-Generated Text in Socio-Technical Contexts

The article by Matthew Hutson (2021) in Nature highlights the rapid advancement of AI-generated text systems (“robo-writers”) and their growing application across domains. These systems, often powered by large language models, present both substantial benefits and notable risks depending on their level of use.

At the administrative level, AI writers offer clear efficiency gains. Tasks such as report drafting, email composition, and summarisation can be automated, reducing cognitive load and operational costs (Davenport and Ronanki, 2018). This aligns with broader machine learning applications aimed at augmenting human productivity. However, risks arise from over-reliance, particularly where outputs are accepted uncritically. Errors, hallucinations, or biases embedded in training data can propagate into decision-making processes (Bender et al., 2021). In high-stakes environments, this introduces technical uncertainty and potential accountability gaps.

At intermediate levels, such as journalism or academic writing support, AI systems can enhance creativity and speed but may blur authorship boundaries. Hutson (2021) notes concern about misinformation and the erosion of trust in written content. This reflects wider debates about epistemic reliability in AI-generated knowledge (Floridi and Chiriatti, 2020).

In creative writing, AI offers novel collaborative possibilities, enabling co-creation and experimentation beyond human limitations. Yet, it raises ethical concerns regarding originality, intellectual property, and cultural homogenisation (Elgammal et al., 2017). The risk is not only technical but also socio-cultural, as machine-generated narratives may replicate dominant patterns in training data.

Critically, the application of AI writers must include risk mitigation strategies, such as transparency mechanisms, human oversight, and dataset auditing. While AI can augment human capabilities, its deployment without robust governance frameworks may undermine trust, creativity, and accountability. Thus, balancing innovation with ethical safeguards is essential in real-world machine learning applications.

Coursework Reflection: CNN Object Recognition and Ethical Implications

This activity involved reviewing a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) model for object recognition and critically reflecting on its technical performance as well as its wider ethical, social, legal and professional implications. The practical task focused on a Jupyter Notebook using the CIFAR-10 dataset, while the reflective component required consideration of how image-recognition systems affect society and professional practice.

The CIFAR-10 dataset contains 60,000 colour images of size 32×32 pixels across ten categories, including dog, cat, automobile, ship and truck. This makes it suitable for introductory computer vision tasks because it is structured, labelled and relatively easy to work with in a teaching context. However, it is also limited because the images are low resolution and simplified compared to real-world visual environments. As a result, good performance on this dataset does not necessarily mean that the model would behave reliably outside the classroom (Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville, 2016).

The CNN model followed a standard supervised learning pipeline. First, the image data was normalised so that pixel values were scaled between 0 and 1. Labels were converted into categorical format so that the model could perform multi-class classification. The architecture then used convolutional layers to detect visual features, max-pooling layers to reduce dimensionality, and dense layers to perform final classification (TensorFlow, 2024; Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville, 2016).

x_train = x_train / 255
x_test = x_test / 255

from tensorflow.keras.utils import to_categorical

y_cat_test = to_categorical(y_test, 10)
from tensorflow.keras.models import Sequential
from tensorflow.keras.layers import Conv2D, MaxPool2D, Flatten, Dense

model = Sequential()
model.add(Conv2D(filters=32, kernel_size=(4,4), input_shape=(32,32,3), activation='relu'))
model.add(MaxPool2D(pool_size=(2,2)))

model.add(Conv2D(filters=64, kernel_size=(4,4), activation='relu'))

A model with this level of performance cannot be considered highly reliable, especially in real-world contexts. The classification behaviour also indicated that some classes were easier to predict than others. Larger and visually clearer object categories, such as ships or automobiles, are often recognised more accurately than categories with overlapping features, such as cats and dogs.

This illustrates one of the key challenges of machine learning: even when overall accuracy appears reasonable, individual decisions can still be wrong.

From a technical perspective, this activity highlighted both the strengths and weaknesses of CNNs. Their main advantage is the ability to automatically learn visual features from raw image data rather than relying on manual feature engineering. This makes them powerful tools for image classification. However, they also operate largely as black-box systems, meaning their internal reasoning is difficult to interpret (Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville, 2016; Krizhevsky, Sutskever and Hinton, 2012).

The ethical and social implications of CNN technology are therefore significant. One key concern is dataset bias. Machine learning models learn patterns from the training data they are given, and if that data is incomplete, unbalanced or socially biased, the model may reproduce and even amplify those inequalities. Although CIFAR-10 is a classroom dataset, the broader issue becomes more serious in real-world applications, where biased image datasets can lead to unfair or discriminatory outcomes (Leslie, 2019).

Privacy is another major concern. CNNs underpin technologies such as facial recognition and automated visual monitoring, which can be used for surveillance in public and private spaces. This raises important legal and ethical questions around consent, proportionality and individual rights. People may be identified or tracked without their knowledge, and errors in recognition can result in reputational harm, exclusion or wrongful suspicion (Martinez-Martin, 2019). In this sense, the technical design of CNN systems cannot be separated from their social impact.

There are also professional responsibilities associated with deploying machine learning systems. Practitioners must evaluate not only model accuracy but also fairness, explainability, robustness and the appropriateness of the dataset. They must communicate limitations clearly and avoid presenting model outputs as objective truth. Legal frameworks such as data protection law reinforce this responsibility by requiring careful handling of personal data and accountability in automated processing (European Parliament and Council of the European Union, 2016).

Overall, this coursework demonstrated that CNNs are highly effective for introductory object recognition tasks, but they also reveal important limitations in accuracy, interpretability and real-world applicability. The exercise strengthened my understanding that responsible machine learning practice requires more than technical implementation. It also requires ethical awareness, critical judgement and professional accountability. The most important lesson from this task is that machine learning models should not be evaluated solely by how well they perform on benchmark datasets, but by how responsibly and fairly they could operate in society.

Group Meeting Report: Airbnb Business Analysis Assessment

This section documents the initial project-planning meeting for the Airbnb Business Analysis Assessment. The purpose of the meeting was to establish a coherent analytical direction, clarify the assessment requirements, and define a business question that was both strategically relevant and methodologically feasible. The discussion focused on aligning the project with the expectations of the Machine Learning module while ensuring that the final output would remain business-oriented, evidence-based and suitable for executive interpretation.

Meeting Purpose

The meeting was used to define the analytical focus of the assessment, confirm the suitability of the dataset and agree a rigorous business question that could be investigated using segmentation, exploratory analysis and machine learning-informed reasoning.

Meeting Overview

Airbnb Business Analysis Assessment within the Machine Learning module.

Assessment Context and Analytical Direction

The team reviewed the assessment brief and confirmed that the submission requires a concise group report supported by relevant visualisations and portfolio artefacts. From the outset, it was recognised that stronger submissions would need to move beyond surface-level description and instead demonstrate justified analytical choices, business relevance and critical interpretation (University of Essex, 2026).

Following discussion, the team agreed that the project should adopt a machine learning-led pricing analysis. This direction was considered appropriate because the Airbnb dataset contains the core variables needed to explore pricing patterns, segment listings and generate actionable recommendations. In particular, the dataset includes variables such as neighbourhood_group, room_type, price and availability, which together provide a strong basis for analysing how listing characteristics may influence market competitiveness.

Final Business Question

What is the optimal price range per neighbourhood_group × room_type segment that maximises booking possibility while preserving competitive host earnings?

This question was selected because it reflects a commercially meaningful problem rather than a purely technical exercise. It places pricing strategy at the centre of the analysis and seeks to balance two competing priorities: improving booking potential and maintaining financially viable returns for hosts. As a result, the question has clear relevance to marketplace decision-making and is capable of producing recommendations that would be meaningful to non-technical stakeholders.

Critical Rationale for Question Selection

Business Relevance

The selected question focuses on pricing, which is a central driver of competitiveness within peer-to-peer accommodation platforms. Identifying optimal price ranges has direct value for both host decision-making and wider platform strategy.

Analytical Feasibility

The structure of the dataset supports segmentation by neighbourhood group and room type, making it possible to compare pricing patterns across distinct market categories in a systematic way.

Machine Learning Alignment

The question fits the module’s emphasis on applied machine learning because it allows exploratory analysis, feature-based comparison and the possible use of predictive methods to strengthen the interpretation of pricing behaviour.

Executive Value

The anticipated outputs can be translated into practical recommendations, such as suggested pricing bands or segment-specific pricing guidance, rather than remaining as purely technical model results.

Proposed Data Strategy

To address the question effectively, the team agreed that the analysis should begin with exploratory data analysis to identify structural patterns, outliers and relationships between price, room type, location and booking-related variables. This stage is necessary to determine whether meaningful pricing differences exist across segments and whether the available data can support robust recommendations.

Preprocessing will also be important. The team identified the need to examine missing values, mitigate extreme pricing outliers and ensure that segment-level comparisons remain interpretable. Because the question relies on the interaction between neighbourhood_group and room_type, segmentation will be central to the analysis rather than treated as a secondary descriptive feature.

Exploratory Analysis Focus

The exploratory stage will examine how prices vary across neighbourhood groups and room types, how availability behaves within each segment, and whether patterns suggest meaningful thresholds or pricing bands associated with stronger booking possibility.

Booking Possibility Proxy

Because direct booking data is not available in the dataset, availability-related variables are expected to be used as a proxy for booking possibility. This introduces interpretive limitations, but it also enables the team to make a reasoned and transparent business inference from the available evidence.

Expected Output

The final output is expected to identify optimal or competitive price ranges for each neighbourhood_group × room_type segment and explain these in a way that supports practical pricing recommendations.

Methodological Reflection

A key strength of the chosen question is that it combines segmentation, business interpretation and machine learning-informed analysis. However, the team also recognised that the concept of “booking possibility” must be operationalised carefully, since the dataset does not directly record booking conversions. This means that the final recommendations will need to be presented with appropriate caution and critical reflection.

Visualisation and Communication Strategy

The team agreed that the final report should prioritise clear, executive-ready visualisations rather than overly technical outputs. Planned visualisations are expected to include price distributions by neighbourhood group, room-type comparisons within segments, and plots showing how pricing varies in relation to availability. These visual outputs will be important for translating analysis into accessible business insight.

This decision reflects an important lesson from machine learning practice: analytical quality is not only determined by model construction, but also by the clarity with which findings are communicated to stakeholders. In this context, visualisation serves both an explanatory and a decision-support function.

Reflection

This meeting was important because it shaped the intellectual direction of the group assessment at an early stage. Rather than beginning immediately with modelling, the discussion emphasised the need to define a strong business problem first. I found this especially valuable because it reinforced that successful machine learning projects are not driven solely by algorithms, but by the quality of the question being asked and the extent to which the analysis remains connected to decision-making needs.

One of the most significant outcomes of the meeting was the shift from a broad pricing idea to a more precise and analytically meaningful question. The final question is stronger because it introduces segmentation explicitly and focuses on the balance between demand potential and host earnings. This makes the task more challenging, but also more relevant and more aligned with the critical expectations of postgraduate-level work.

The meeting also highlighted the importance of methodological caution. Although the dataset appears well suited to pricing analysis, it does not include direct booking outcomes. As a result, any reference to booking possibility must be inferred through proxy indicators rather than measured directly. This was a useful reminder that machine learning and business analytics often involve working within data limitations, and that professional integrity requires those limitations to be acknowledged clearly.

Overall, this planning process strengthened my understanding of how machine learning projects should be framed in practice. It showed that rigorous analytical work begins with problem definition, critical feasibility assessment and thoughtful interpretation of what the data can and cannot support. In this sense, the meeting contributed not only to the group project itself, but also to my wider development in structured, evidence-based analytical thinking.

This has strengthened my ability to move beyond surface-level interpretation and engage with machine learning results in a more rigorous and reflective way.
