Coursework Reflection: CNN Object Recognition and Ethical Implications

This activity involved reviewing a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) model for object recognition and critically reflecting on its technical performance as well as its wider ethical, social, legal and professional implications. The practical task focused on a Jupyter Notebook using the CIFAR-10 dataset, while the reflective component required consideration of how image-recognition systems affect society and professional practice.

The CIFAR-10 dataset contains 60,000 colour images of size 32×32 pixels across ten categories, including dog, cat, automobile, ship and truck. This makes it suitable for introductory computer vision tasks because it is structured, labelled and relatively easy to work with in a teaching context. However, it is also limited because the images are low resolution and simplified compared to real-world visual environments. As a result, good performance on this dataset does not necessarily mean that the model would behave reliably outside the classroom.

The CNN model followed a standard supervised learning pipeline. First, the image data was normalised so that pixel values were scaled between 0 and 1. Labels were converted into categorical format so that the model could perform multi-class classification. The architecture then used convolutional layers to detect visual features, max-pooling layers to reduce dimensionality, and dense layers to perform final classification.

model.compile(loss='categorical_crossentropy', optimizer='adam', metrics=['accuracy'])

The recorded notebook results showed a test accuracy of approximately 67%, which is acceptable for a simple baseline CNN but still highlights clear limitations. A model with this level of performance cannot be considered highly reliable, especially in real-world contexts. The classification behaviour also indicated that some classes were easier to predict than others. Larger and visually clearer object categories, such as ships or automobiles, are often recognised more accurately than categories with overlapping features, such as cats and dogs.

The original notebook output showed that image index 16 was predicted correctly as a dog. Changing the index provides insight into how robust the model is across different samples. In some cases, the model predicts correctly when the visual features are distinctive. In other cases, it misclassifies images where the object is ambiguous, small or visually similar to another category. This illustrates one of the key challenges of machine learning: even when overall accuracy appears reasonable, individual decisions can still be wrong.

From a technical perspective, this activity highlighted both the strengths and weaknesses of CNNs. Their main advantage is the ability to automatically learn visual features from raw image data rather than relying on manual feature engineering. This makes them powerful tools for image classification. However, they also operate largely as black-box systems, meaning their internal reasoning is difficult to interpret. This lack of transparency becomes especially important in high-stakes uses of computer vision, such as facial recognition, healthcare diagnostics or surveillance systems.

The ethical and social implications of CNN technology are therefore significant. One key concern is dataset bias. Machine learning models learn patterns from the training data they are given, and if that data is incomplete, unbalanced or socially biased, the model may reproduce and even amplify those inequalities. Although CIFAR-10 is a classroom dataset, the broader issue becomes more serious in real-world applications, where biased image datasets can lead to unfair or discriminatory outcomes.

Privacy is another major concern. CNNs underpin technologies such as facial recognition and automated visual monitoring, which can be used for surveillance in public and private spaces. This raises important legal and ethical questions around consent, proportionality and individual rights. People may be identified or tracked without their knowledge, and errors in recognition can result in reputational harm, exclusion or wrongful suspicion. In this sense, the technical design of CNN systems cannot be separated from their social impact.

There are also professional responsibilities associated with deploying machine learning systems. Practitioners must evaluate not only model accuracy but also fairness, explainability, robustness and the appropriateness of the dataset. They must communicate limitations clearly and avoid presenting model outputs as objective truth. Legal frameworks such as data protection law reinforce this responsibility by requiring careful handling of personal data and accountability in automated processing.

Overall, this coursework demonstrated that CNNs are highly effective for introductory object recognition tasks, but they also reveal important limitations in accuracy, interpretability and real-world applicability. The exercise strengthened my understanding that responsible machine learning practice requires more than technical implementation. It also requires ethical awareness, critical judgement and professional accountability. The most important lesson from this task is that machine learning models should not be evaluated solely by how well they perform on benchmark datasets, but by how responsibly and fairly they could operate in society.
